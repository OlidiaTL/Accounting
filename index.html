DOUBLE-ENTRY BOOKKEEPING

Double-Entry Records for Expenses and Incomes

Income

Income is the money that a business or individual earns. This can come from various sources, such as:

  • Sales Revenue: Money received from selling goods or services.
  • Interest Income: Money earned from savings accounts or investments.
  • Rent: Money received from leasing property.

Expense

Expense is the money that a business or individual spends. This includes costs incurred to operate or maintain activities, such as:

  • Rent: Money paid for using a space (like an office).
  • Salaries: Money paid to employees for their work.
  • Utilities: Payments for services like electricity, water, and internet.

In simple terms, income is what you earn, and expenses are what you spend.

An account is opened for each type of expense and income.

DOUBLE ENTRY RECORDS FOR DRAWINGS

Drawings Definition: Value taken by the owner from the business for personal use.
-Form of Drawings: Money, non-current assets, or goods.
- Recording Drawings: Any Drawings - Debit drawings account, credit cash/bank or relevant asset account.
- At the End of the Financial Year: Drawings account transferred to the Debit of the capital account, reducing the amount owed by the business to the owner.
NOTE:
When goods are withdrawn, the purchases account is credited, not the goods account.
This is because the goods were originally purchased for resale, and the amount of goods available for resale is reduced when the goods are taken by the owner.

Balancing Ledger Accounts

Balancing a ledger account is an essential task in bookkeeping to ensure that the debits and credits are equal. Here are the steps to balance a ledger account:

Instructions to Balance a Ledger Account

  1. Prepare Your Ledger
    • Gather all transactions related to the account you want to balance.
    • Ensure you have the opening balance for the period.
  2. Record Transactions
    • List all debits (money coming in) and credits (money going out) in the ledger account.
    • Each transaction should have a date, description, and amount.
  3. Calculate Total Debits
    • Add up all the debit entries.
    • Write the total at the bottom of the debit side.
  4. Calculate Total Credits
    • Add up all the credit entries.
    • Write the total at the bottom of the credit side.
  1. Determine the Difference
    • Subtract the total credits from the total debits:
      • If total debits > total credits, you have a debit balance.
      • If total credits > total debits, you have a credit balance.
  2. Enter the Balance
    • Write the difference as a balance on the side that is lower (debit or credit) to equalize the two sides.
    • Label this entry as "Balance c/d" (carried down) with the date.
  3. Close the Account
    • At the end of the period, carry the balance forward to the next period.
    • Write this as "Balance b/d" (brought down) in the new period's ledger.
  4. Review for Accuracy
    • Double-check all calculations and entries for accuracy.
    • Ensure that the total debits equal total credits after entering the balance.

DOUBLE-ENTRY RECORDS FOR SALES, PURCHASES, AND RETURNS

1. Double Entry Accounting Principle

  • Every transaction affects at least two accounts.
  • Debits must equal credits, maintaining the accounting equation:
    • Assets = Liabilities + Equity

2. Sales Transactions

Sales of Goods: For Cash or Cheque – money is received immediately as the goods are sold in the form of cash or a cheque
  • Debit: Cash or Bank Account (increase in assets)
  • Credit: Sales Revenue (increase in income)
Example Entry:
If goods are sold for cash $100:
  • Debit Cash Account $100
  • Credit Sales Revenue Account: $100
If goods are sold, a cheque of $300 is received:
  • Debit Bank Account $300
  • Credit Sales Revenue Account: $300
Sales of Goods: On Credit – goods sold but money to be received at a later date
  • Debit: Trade receivable (increase in assets)
  • Credit: Sales Revenue (increase in income)

3. Purchases Transactions

Purchases of Goods:
  • Debit: Purchases Account (increase in expenses)
  • Credit: Cash or Trade Payable (decrease in assets or increase in liabilities)

Goods purchased and paid for in cash or by cheque - money is paid immediately as the goods are purchased in the form of cash or a cheque

Example Entry:
If goods are purchased for $50 Cash:
  • Debit Purchases: $50
  • Credit Cash $50
If goods are purchased and paid for by cheque $50:
  • Debit Purchases Account: $50
  • Credit Bank Account $50
Purchases of Goods: On Credit – goods bought but money to be paid at a later date
  • Debit: Purchases (increase in assets)
  • Credit: Trade Payable (increase in income)

4. Returns Transactions

Definitions

  • Sales Returns/Returns Inwards refer to goods that customers return to the seller after a purchase, usually due to defects, dissatisfaction, or incorrect items.
  • Purchases Returns/Returns outwards occur when a business returns goods it has purchased from suppliers, typically due to defects, excess inventory, or incorrect items.
Sales Returns:
  • Debit: Sales Returns Account (decrease in income)
  • Credit: Trade Receivable (decrease in assets)
Example Entry:
If a customer returns goods worth $20:
  • Debit Sales Returns: $20
  • Credit Trade Receivable: $20
Purchases Returns/ returns outwards:
  • Debit: Trade Payable account (decrease in liabilities)
  • Credit: Purchases Returns Account (decrease in expenses)
Example Entry:
If goods worth $15 are returned to the supplier:
  • Debit Trade Payable: $15
  • Credit Purchases Returns: $15

5. Importance of Double Entry System

  • Provides a complete record of financial transactions.
  • Helps in detecting errors and ensuring accuracy.
  • Facilitates the preparation of financial statements.

6. Summary

  • Each sale and purchase impacts both revenue and expense accounts.
  • Returns adjust both sales and purchases, ensuring accurate reporting in financial statements.

Double-entry records for Carriage inwards and carriage outwards

Definitions

Carriage – Is the cost of transporting goods

Types of Carriages

Carriage Inwards
-It is the cost of bringing the goods to the business.

Carriage outwards
-It is the cost of delivering the goods to the customer.

Accounting Treatment

Journal Entries:
  • Debit: Carriage Inwards Account (increases expenses)
  • Credit: Cash or Accounts Payable (decreases assets or increases liabilities)
Example
If carriage inwards is $100:
  • Entry:
    • Debit Carriage Inwards: $100
    • Credit Cash/Accounts Payable: $100

Carriage Outwards

Accounting Treatment
  • Debit: Carriage Outwards Account (increases expenses)
  • Credit: Cash or Trade Payable (decreases assets or increases liabilities)
Example
If carriage outwards is $50:
  • Entry:
    • Debit Carriage Outwards: $50
    • Credit Cash/Trade Payable: $50

Summary

  • Carriage Inwards increases expenses related to incoming goods, affecting the cost of goods sold.
  • Carriage Outwards increases expenses related to delivering goods, affecting overall operating expenses.
  • Both are recorded as debits in their respective accounts and credits to cash or accounts payable.
